Case Study: Skeleton Loader

Overview

To demonstrate the in-depth, integrative UX research process we performed on Radiant, let's examine a single component up-close.

Radiant used an agile sprint framework. Our PMs utilized a __ system: once we approved one component, we could pull a new one from the backlog. Sometimes a component existed in the 1.0 design system, and initial research could reference what already existed in the system while incorporating new research and best practices. However, sometimes a component was entirely new, and we started from scratch. One such example was the skeleton loader: an unusually liminal component that appears during load times. The first stage is always research. How do other web apps leverage skeleton loaders (aka --, --)? I switched on airplane mode and began experimenting with my phone, opening apps in my library and examining how they visualized loading content. I began identifying distinctions between when we might use a skeleton loader versus when a spinner could suffice.

Discover use case

Loading full pages of content in between experiences: for instance, initially loading the app’s landing page (AC Home), or switching from AC Home into Spend Analyzer.

Skeleton loaders likely need not be used within an experience (for instance, after submitting form input for Student Card Hub), or every time a page is loaded (returning from AC Home after submitting a card payment), as the wait times for these tasks are typically quicker due to fewer API calls

Field Research

I identified a few recurring variables: motion, accessibility, and geometry. I've broken them down here in depth.

Animation: Pulse or wave?

  • Many major design systems animate their skeleton loader via a pulse, radiating from the center, or a wave, swooping across the screen as a line.
  • In a study conducted by UX Collective, 60% of user’s guessed that any animation presented a shorter time duration.
  • 68% of users guessed that a wave animation represented a shorter time duration than a pulse, and than a left to right animation was brief than right to left.
  • This may be because of our natural eye patterns, since (many) languages are written left-right, top-down. A linear placeholder animation leads to reduced cognitive load.
  • Staggering in: UX Collective recommends that as soon as content is available, it should populate, rather than swap the skeleton loader for content once all page content is loaded.

Color Contrast

Color contrast difference between a skeleton loader exploration for DDLS 2.0 with WCAG-compliant 3:1 contrast, and existing color contrast in Material Design’s skeleton loader

  • There was ambiguity surrounding whether skeleton loaders are considered active or inactive components, and therefore whether or not they are required to meet a certain contrast requirement
  • The overwhelming majority of skeleton loaders in other design systems had a ratio below 3:1, which does not meet the requirements for graphical elements.
  • UX blogger Adrian Roselli argues that most skeletons are inaccessible by design, as they convey a passive loading state.
  • WCAG Guidance 1.4.11: “Visual information required to identify user interface components and states...[must have] a color contrast ratio of at least 3:1”
  • The overwhelming majority of skeleton loaders in other design systems had a ratio below 3:1, which does not meet the requirements for graphical elements
The takeaway: For unloaded/disabled content, high contrast goes against user expectations. A WCAG-compliant skeleton may be mistaken for fully loaded interactive content. This disconnect is especially true if there is noticeable discrepancy from the standard set by other apps.

Geometry

Google’s Material Design, REI’s Cedar Design, and Ant Design set intentional barriers to fidelity.

Ant Design creates placeholder states for avatar visuals, as well as paragraph and headline text. The height and width of these placeholders can be modified, and there are toggles for corner radii, height and width, row count (for multiple lines of text), and a boolean for whether a given component should appear as a loading placeholder.

Cedar Design provides UI presets: default, heading, line, rectangle, and square. Cedar recommends using presets in a way that reflects page content but does not indicate “specific interactive elements like a standalone button or image.”

Like Cedar, Material Design provides a handful of presets built into components as loading states. These states are accompanied by guidance advocating they be used primarily to reflect the pages visual hierarchy.

In summary:

  • Skeleton loaders add visual complexity to reduce dropoff during long load times.
  • They don’t need to meet the same color contrast standards as other components.
  • Different design systems use a gradient of fidelity and complexity in loading states, so we need to choose how faithful we want our loader to be to our wireframes.
