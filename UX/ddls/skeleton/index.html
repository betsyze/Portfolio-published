To demonstrate the in-depth, integrative UX research process we performed on Radiant, let's examine a single component up-close.

The process:

Radiant used an agile sprint framework. Once we handed off final design components after a sprint, our PMs could pull a cluster of new component stories from a thorough backlog. Sometimes a component existed in the 1.0 design system, and initial research could reference what already existed in the system while incorporating new research and best practices. Othertimes, design system components were net new, and we started from scratch.

One such example was the skeleton loader: an unusually liminal component that appears during load times.

Getting started

Research

The first stage of component design is always field research. How do other web apps leverage skeleton loaders (aka Ghost Chip, or just “Skeleton”)? During comparative research, I started to identify distinctions between when we might use a skeleton loader versus when a spinner could suffice

Big 4 Design Systems

The "Big 4" design systems (Material, Fluent, Carbon, and Apple Human Interface Guidelines) are a helpful jumping off point, as leaders in the industry that have already done a great deal of research and testing on best practices for skeleton loader design and implementation. make varied use of skeleton loaders.

I got smart on skeletons by jotting down notes and observations from public documentation by leaders in the field.

Client use case:

Based on industry expectations and existing app architecture, I determined this component is needed for loading full pages of dynamic content in between experiences: for instance, initially loading the app’s landing page (AC Home), or switching from AC Home into the budget-tracking feature Spend Analyzer.

Pulse or wave?

Many major design systems animate their skeleton loader via pulse or wave. In fact, some design systems, like Fluent, account for both. We wanted to make a single recommendation for a component that would have limited usage.

In a study conducted by UX Collective, 68% of users guessed that a wave animation represented a shorter time duration than a pulse, and than a left to right animation was brief than right to left This may be because our natural eye patterns (most) languages are written left-right, top-down, leading to less cognitive load

In the same study, UX Collective also recommends that as soon as content is available, skeleton should populate, rather than swap the skeleton loader for content once all page content is loaded. This diverges from Carbon's recommended pattern of keeping the skeleton loader in place until all content is loaded.

Our motion recommendation

We determined that a wave animation would be the best fit for our skeleton loader. I advised Pete, our UI designer, that the loader should animate as a wave from top-to-bottom, left-to-right, using grayscale geometry.

Color contrast

Our research also accounted for potential accessibility questions.

There is current ambiguity surrounding whether skeleton loaders are considered active or inactive components, and therefore whether or not they are required to meet a certain contrast requirement.

Field notes: The overwhelming majority of skeleton loaders in other design systems have a ratio below 3:1, which does not meet the requirements for graphical elements. UX blogger Adrian Roselli argues that most skeleton’s are inaccessible as they convey a loading state of active components

WCAG Guidance 1.4.11 (Non-text contrast): “Visual information required to identify user interface components and states...[must have] a color contrast ratio of at least 3:1”

The overwhelming majority of skeleton loaders in commonly used apps and design systems have a ratio below 3:1, which does not meet the requirements for graphical elements.

Our color contrast recommendations

Harsh contrast for an inactive skeleton goes against user expectations. The skeleton may be mistaken for loaded and thus interactive content.

This is especially true if there is noticeable discrepancy from the standard set by other apps. Therefore, the skeleton loader can be considered an inactive component, and color styles do not need to adhere to visual accessibility guidelines.

Build

Early designs

Once we had a clear recommendation for the design of the skeleton loader, we built out the component in Figma. In accordance with our design system's guidelines, we created a document detailing the component's default state, usage, anatomy, variants, sizing, positioning, and motion. (Behavior=not relevant for this component.)

I tag-teamed with a UI designer to initially document purely visual specifications. After client approval, I would later begin accessibility on the second-round approved design.

Final Documentation

Learnings and Takeaways

Alongside my UI support, I presented three times to Ravi and Discover's internal design system team: once for research, once for R1 designs, and once for final design and accessibility documentation.

Our design was launched and Discover developers built our skeleton loader into their official Storybook. Development began on the loader's inclusion in the app's AC Home page.

Also, personal win: this was the first component I took ownership of, and it was a challenge. The skeleton loader diverges in several ways from traditional design system components, so I was onboarded to the Radiant Design System at the same time I was learning how and when to break it.

Fortunately, the design system team both internally at FCB and externally at Discover was supportive of my approach. I was able to articulate my decisions and process to a degree of detail I had never before attempted, and I'm proud of what I achieved.

After final approval, I took on 10 more component research, design, and accessibility tasks before FCB's work was paused following Discover's acquisition by Capital One. These components included a file uploader, form elements such as dropdown menus and radio buttons, content view control elements like such as tabs.
